What is it?

Depression (Major depressive disorder) is a common and serious mental health condition which involves loss of interest in activities that were previously enjoyable, feeling worthless or excessively guilty, sleeping little or too much and in extreme cases, there can be thoughts of self-harm. With nearly three in ten adults (29%) being diagnosed with a form of depression at some point and 18% (according to a national survey) currently experiencing it, it has become a severe issue in many peoples' lives today. Depression can occur at nearly any age.

References: Psychiatry.org, Gallup Poll

Prevalence?

One of the nations most impacted by the rising prevalence of anxiety and associated mental health disorders is Australia. With approximately 17% of Australians diagnosed with an anxiety disorder, the problem is still getting worse despite continuous national initiatives and raised awareness. These difficulties have been made worse by the COVID-19 pandemic's long-term effects, which have increased stress, social isolation, and uncertainty. Effective prevention and treatment strategies are desperately needed nationwide, as evidenced by research showing that anxiety disorders are not only very common but can also play a significant role in the onset of depression.

References: ABS Mental Health Study, Healthdata.org

Signs & Symptoms?

  • Persistent sadness or emptiness
  • Significant changes in appetite or development of eating disorders
  • Increased fatigue from even small workouts
  • Powerful mood swings
  • Difficulty with concentration

Depression can vary a lot with everyone who experiences it, with many different signs. The best thing you can do is to stay vigilant as soon as something happens and consult a professional.

Long-term effects: Untreated depression may lead to chronic health issues, social isolation, substance dependence, and difficulty in maintaining education or employment. These effects can last for years or even a lifetime without intervention.

Reference: Beyond Blue

Risk Factors / Causes?

  • Genetics: Depression can run through families, creating a predisposition, though not guaranteed.
  • Brain Chemistry: Imbalances in serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine can increase risk.
  • Social Factors: Traumatic events, loss, physical trauma, and substance use can contribute.

Treatments / Strategies?

Depression is treated with a combination of strategies, often involving professional medical treatments such as antidepressants and brain stimulation therapies.

Where to Go for Help?

If you or someone you know is struggling with depression, support is available.

  • Emergency: Call 000 if urgent help is needed.
  • 24/7 helplines: 13 11 14 (crisis support), 1300 22 4636 (Beyond Blue for depression and anxiety).
  • Seek professional support from counselors, doctors (GPs), and therapists.
  • Online resources: MindSpot Clinic, Black Dog Institute, Beyond Blue
